THE WEATHER BOX

2018

Light | Motion | Responsive | Animation | Digital

Individual Project

It's a box. It's a light box. It displays weather patterns in animation and it responds to people's motion. By holding the device in different ways, the user could see rain, snow and lightning in different perspectives.

CONCEPT

The project started with the idea of giving light some sort of form that makes it feel tangible and corporeal. There are some precedent projects that make the interaction with light really fun. Like Light Kinetic by Espadaysantacruz Studio, The Play of Brilliants by Nipek, and LED Matrix Sand Toy by Ruiz Brothers.

Inspired by these projects, I started designing a light box that responds to motion. I chose weather patterns to be the subject of the animation because our familiarity with them would make the light animation recognizable but refreshing to our senses. They also lend themselves to having views from different perspectives, which would make the connection between the display and people's motion very natural.

PROCESS

After some research on digital electronic devices, I decided to use a Raspberry Pi and connect a 32x32 LED matrix and a 6-axis accelerometer/gyroscope to it. It was my first time using a Raspberry Pi so it took me some effort to set it up. I started writing Processing sketches for the weather animations and displayed the graphics on the LED matrix using the LED-matrix library created by Henner Zeller. Then I used the MPU6050 accelerometer/gyroscope to gather the orientation and rotation data. Integrating the code for the animation and the measurement, I was able to associate the change of the animation with the movement of the device.

For each type of weather, there are three perspectives: sky view, side view, and ground view. As I was writing the code, I looked up videos for references of different weather patterns in motion. After a lot of tweaking and testing, I ended up with animations for rain, snow and lightning.

800 lines of code in Processing

While working on the animation on the screen, I was also thinking about the casing for the box and the diffuser for the light. To keep it simple and clean, I built a wooden box to enclose all the electronic parts and fit the LED panel in. Then I placed a piece of translucent acrylic on top of the light to diffuse it. I tried different materials for the diffuser to achieve an ideal effect. The acrylic is attached to the box with screws and nuts so that the distance between the acrylic and the LED could be adjusted to achieve various degrees of diffusion. Originally I was using buttons for switches for the different weather modes, but then I decided to make all the controls completely motion-based, to achieve a more coherent interaction experience.

EXHIBITION

The project was shown at a school exhibition. People were prompted to pick up the box and play with it.
